His tenure, particularly under President Bush, was marked by the controversial Iraq War and the allegations of weapons of mass destruction that became a defining moment of the early 21st-century American foreign policy.

Rumsfeld's political career was illustrious and complex, spanning various capacities and presidential administrations. Serving twice as Defense Secretary, he was the youngest to hold the post under Ford and the oldest under Bush. His second stint at the Pentagon was notably defined by the aftermath of the September 11 attacks and the subsequent wars in Afghanistan and Iraq.

A key figure in the decision to invade Iraq in 2003, Rumsfeld was instrumental in persuading the American public and international allies of the presence of weapons of mass destruction in Iraq, a claim that was later debunked, as no such weapons were found. This decision and the justification for the war have been subjects of intense debate and criticism.

Rumsfeld's tenure was not without scandal; most notably, the Abu Ghraib prisoner abuse incident cast a long shadow over American military operations abroad. The leaked images of Iraqi prisoners being mistreated by U.S. soldiers drew global condemnation and raised questions about the oversight and ethics of the U.S. military and its leadership.

Further controversy surrounded Rumsfeld for authorizing harsh interrogation techniques on detainees, which many international observers and human rights organizations condemned as torture. These actions, in part, tarnished America's image on the global stage and ignited debates over the balance between national security and human rights.

A known close ally of Vice President Dick Cheney, Rumsfeld's approach to the Iraq war and its aftermath drew criticism from various quarters. His management style, often described as brash and uncompromising, became a focal point for critics who blamed him for strategic errors in both Iraq and Afghanistan, arguing they contributed to the protracted nature of both conflicts.

President George W. Bush eventually replaced Rumsfeld in November 2006 amid dwindling support for the Iraq War and growing public criticism of Rumsfeld's handling of the conflict. This decision marked a significant moment in Bush's presidency, acknowledging the contentious nature of Rumsfeld's tenure and the desire for a new direction in American military strategy.

Critics have also pointed to Rumsfeld's decisions and management style as factors contributing to the instability and violence in post-invasion Iraq. His insistence on a smaller, more agile military force was contentious, with detractors arguing it lacked the manpower for effective peacekeeping and nation-building.

Beyond the controversies, Rumsfeld had a diverse career. Before his high-profile roles in government, he was a successful businessman, serving as CEO of several companies, and he even served as a Navy pilot in his early years. His deep involvement in politics also saw him serve as a U.S. NATO ambassador, among other roles.

Rumsfeld's complex legacy in American political and military history is underpinned by his undeniable impact on global geopolitics in the early 21st century. His role in shaping U.S. foreign policy, particularly in relation to the Iraq War, continues to be a subject of analysis and debate.

While his decisions and the outcomes of the Iraq and Afghanistan wars are contentious, his contributions to American public service over several decades reflect a deeply engaged and influential figure in U.S. politics.

Donald Rumsfeld's death marks the end of an era for American defense and foreign policy, leaving behind a legacy interwoven with some of the most significant and divisive moments in recent American history. His impact on global affairs, for better or worse, will be remembered and debated for years to come.
