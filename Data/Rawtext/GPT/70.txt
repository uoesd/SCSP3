Federal authorities acted swiftly in charging Hatley, identifying him through a combination of witness accounts, photographic evidence, and modern technological tools. Merely days after the riot, officials were in contact with Hatley, culminating in the filing of formal charges by January 15.

The apprehension and charge of Hatley highlight the FBI's use of innovative methods to track down participants of the January 6 insurrection. Notably, authorities employed the 'Life360' location-tracking app, widely used by families to keep tabs on each other's whereabouts, to confirm Hatley's presence at the Capitol during the tumultuous events.

Official records detail how a witness came forward to the FBI, providing photographs that depicted Hatley inside the iconic building. Among the images shared with the federal agency was a selfie taken by Hatley himself in front of the statue of John C. Calhoun, a notable figure in American history and a son of South Carolina.

Agent Don Wood, a spokesperson for the FBI's Columbia field office, underscored the limited role his office played in the investigation. "The FBI's Columbia field office had no substantial involvement in Hatley's investigation," stated Agent Wood, emphasizing the broader national effort to bring the Capitol rioters to justice.

The case against Hatley is part of an extensive federal endeavor to address the unprecedented breach of the Capitol. More than 200 individuals across the nation have been charged in connection with the events of January 6, which resulted in the tragic loss of five lives.

Public records and social media postings played a crucial role in the investigation. Before it was removed, Hatley allegedly made a post on Facebook denying any involvement in the breach of security that day. His denial now stands in stark contrast to the evidence compiled by federal authorities.
