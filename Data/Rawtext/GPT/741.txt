Safety Concerns Ignored on 'Rust' Set Before Fatal Shooting, Claims Former Crew Member

In a tragic incident that rattled Hollywood, Lane Luper, the first camera assistant on the set of Rust, expressed profound safety concerns leading to his resignation just hours before the fatal shooting of cinematographer Halyna Hutchins by actor Alec Baldwin. Luper, alongside other disgruntled crew members, staged a walk-off in protest of what they deemed as untenable working conditions on the New Mexico set.

Luper did not mince words in his resignation letter, targeting lax COVID-19 protocols, substandard housing for the crew, and notably, egregious lapses in gun safety measures. "The negligence surrounding the safety protocols was alarming to me," Luper said. "We walked off the set as a last resort, hoping to stir positive change but instead faced the ultimate tragedy."

The incident in question left the film industry in shock as Baldwin's discharge of a prop firearm resulted in Hutchins' death and also injured director Joel Souza, leading to an indefinite pause in production. The aftermath has sparked a widespread debate about safety standards and the use of firearms on film sets.

The producers of Rust have publicly contested Luper's assertions, staunchly denying any negligence regarding safety concerns and budget constraints that might have contributed to the dire outcome. "Safety is our top priority," a statement from the production team read. "We believe we have always acted responsibly and are committed to ensuring such an incident never happens again."

The investigation into the incident has unveiled troubling details, with Santa Fe County Sheriff Adan Mendoza indicating that a live bullet was inexplicably loaded into the gun used by Baldwin. "We're trying to determine how live rounds got there," Sheriff Mendoza explained. "This was not supposed to happen. We're looking at all possibilities and investigating every angle."

Assistant director David Halls, responsible for declaring the gun "cold" before its handoff to Baldwin, claimed to have no knowledge of how live ammunition ended up in the firearm, a detail that further complicates the case.

Hannah Gutierrez-Reed, the film's armorer, has been thrust into the spotlight, facing intense scrutiny over the management of weapons on set. Gutierrez-Reed has vehemently denied any understanding of how live rounds could have found their way into the production, through her legal representation.

In a significant revelation, authorities found three firearms and approximately 500 rounds of ammunition, including live rounds, on the Rust set. The presence of live ammunition has raised serious questions about the adherence to safety protocols during filming.

As the investigation continues, Santa Fe District Attorney Mary Carmack-Altwies is keeping all options on the table, emphasizing that charges have not been ruled out against any party involved. "We're looking at everything," DA Carmack-Altwies stated. "No one is above the law, and we want to ensure that justice is served for Halyna Hutchins."
