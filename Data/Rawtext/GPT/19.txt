Washington Tightens Security for Biden's Inauguration After Capitol Riot

Washington, DC â€“ Following the violence at the Capitol on January 6 that left five people dead, Washington, DC Mayor Muriel Bowser is taking no chances for President-elect Joe Biden's inauguration. In a determined move to prevent a repeat of the chaos, Bowser has reached out to the US Department of Homeland Security (DHS) requesting a significant boost in security measures for the upcoming presidential inauguration.

In her communication with the DHS, Mayor Bowser emphasized that the tragic events of January 6 call for a reevaluation of security protocols. "Given the horrifying events at our Capitol, it is imperative we take a different approach to security for the inauguration," Bowser stated, underlining the urgency of her request.

The siege of the Capitol building by supporters of outgoing President Donald Trump not only disrupted the congressional session to certify Biden's election victory but also resulted in the deaths of five individuals, including a police officer. The violence has since necessitated a reexamination of security protocols, with Bowser at the forefront of advocating for heightened measures.

As part of her comprehensive security proposal, the Mayor has asked for daily intelligence and threat briefings from the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI). Additionally, she has called for the cancellation of all public gathering permits in the period spanning January 11 to January 24, aiming to minimize opportunities for violence.

Bowser's request extends to a coordinated effort among several key departments including Homeland Security, Defense, Justice, as well as Congress and the Supreme Court. The unified deployment of federal forces, she argues, is crucial in ensuring safety.

Senate Democratic Leader Chuck Schumer echoed Bowser's concerns, noting, "As the inauguration approaches, the threat from violent extremist groups remains high." Schumer's comments underscore the palpable tension and security concerns surrounding the upcoming event.

Further ramping up efforts to address the fallout from the Capitol riot, the US Department of Justice has charged two more alleged participants. The move is part of an ongoing crackdown on those responsible for the violence.

Across the country, police and fire departments are conducting investigations into their own members who might have taken part in the riot, reflecting the widespread impact of the events and the urgent need for accountability.

Adding to concerns, US Army Secretary Ryan McCarthy informed US Representative Jason Crow that as many as 25 domestic terrorism cases have been opened in relation to the riot. Crow relayed the information, highlighting the gravity of the situation and the threat posed by domestic terrorism.

In a departure from tradition, President Trump has announced he will not attend Biden's inauguration, which is set to be a scaled-down event owing to the COVID-19 pandemic, bearing semblance to the Democratic National Convention.

As the nation prepares for the presidential transition, the emphasis on security has never been more acute, with officials at all levels working diligently to ensure a peaceful and orderly inauguration day.
