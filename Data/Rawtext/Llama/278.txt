COVID-19 Cases Rise Again in US, Delta Variant and Low Vaccination Rates Blamed

The number of COVID-19 cases in the United States is increasing once again, with new cases per day doubling over the past three weeks, according to data from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). The rise in cases is attributed to the delta variant, low vaccination rates, and Fourth of July gatherings.

Confirmed infections have climbed to an average of about 23,600 a day, a significant increase from the average of 11,000 cases per day in early June. However, deaths remain lower than the peak in January, highlighting the effectiveness of the COVID-19 vaccines.

Dr. Bill Powderly, co-director of the Infectious-Disease Division at Washington University's School of Medicine in St. Louis, said, "We're seeing a significant increase in cases, and it's clearly related to the delta variant and low vaccination rates in some areas."

States with lower vaccination rates are seeing bigger jumps in cases. Mississippi and Louisiana, for example, have low vaccination rates and are facing significant rises in cases. Dr. James Lawler, leader of the Global Center for Health Security at the University of Nebraska Medical Center in Omaha, said, "We're seeing a perfect storm of factors contributing to the rise in cases."

Health authorities in some areas are urging even vaccinated people to wear masks again, as the delta variant is highly contagious and can spread easily through the air. Erik Frederick, chief administrative officer at Mercy Hospital Springfield, said, "We're taking precautions to protect our patients and staff, and we're encouraging everyone to wear masks in public places."

There is resistance in some states to reintroducing restrictions or preventive measures, but health experts say it's crucial to take action to prevent the spread of the virus. President Biden is boosting vaccination efforts among young people with the help of celebrity Olivia Rodrigo.
