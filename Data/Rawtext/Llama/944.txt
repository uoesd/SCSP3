Impeachment Precedent Exists for Trying Federal Officials After Leaving Office

A historical precedent for impeaching and trying a federal official after they have left office exists, and it may have implications for the ongoing impeachment trial of former President Donald Trump. The case of Senator William Blount, who was impeached and tried after being expelled from the Senate, provides a relevant example.

Blount was a U.S. senator from Tennessee who was impeached in 1797 for planning an attack on Spanish territories to benefit Great Britain. His impeachment marked the Senate's first impeachment case. Although Blount was initially expelled from the Senate, he was still tried after leaving office.

According to legal experts, an officeholder can be tried after leaving office, and being impeached does not signal the end of a political career. "The Senate has the flexibility to conduct an impeachment trial whenever it chooses," said Stewart Harris, a constitutional law professor and Blount Mansion board member at Lincoln Memorial University.

Blount's case was eventually dismissed by the Senate on jurisdictional grounds, and he did not attend his trial. However, his political career continued in Tennessee after his impeachment. He was elected to the state legislature and served as a judge, indicating that impeachment may not permanently harm a political figure's career.

Trump's supporters argue that impeachment could prevent him from holding future federal office. However, Blount's case does not directly support this theory. "Impeachment does not necessarily terminate a politician's career," said Dr. Gregory Ablavsky, a history professor at Stanford University.

Another precedent for post-office impeachment is the case of Secretary of War William Belknap, who was impeached after resigning for bribery. Belknap's case suggests that impeachment can still be pursued even after an official has left office.

Historians and legal experts caution that impeachment does not necessarily mean the end of a political career. Blount's continued popularity and political involvement after his impeachment support this notion. "Impeachment is not a lifetime ban from politics," said Jonathan Turley, a professor at George Washington University.

While Blount's case was dismissed on jurisdictional grounds, it does not necessarily mean that a political career is over after impeachment.
