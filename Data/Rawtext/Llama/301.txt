COVID-19 Vaccines Proven Safe and Effective in Real-World Setting, Study Confirms

A recent study published in The New England Journal of Medicine has confirmed the safety and effectiveness of COVID-19 vaccines in a real-world setting. The study, conducted by researchers from Clalit Research Institute, Harvard Medical School, and the Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health, analyzed data from nearly 2 million individuals in Israel and found few adverse events associated with the Pfizer/BioNTech BNT162b2 mRNA vaccine.

According to Ben Reis, HMS assistant professor of pediatrics and director of the predictive medicine group at Boston Children's, "This study is likely to help with vaccine hesitancy issues by offering comprehensive safety information." Ran Balicer, director of the Clalit Research Institute and chairman of Israel's National Expert Advisory Team on COVID-19 response, emphasized the safety of mRNA vaccines and the higher risks of 'natural' coronavirus morbidity.

The study found that myocarditis was identified as a rare side effect of the vaccine, with a rate of 2.7 excess cases per 100,000 vaccinated people. However, the risk of myocarditis and other serious conditions was significantly higher among unvaccinated individuals infected with SARS-CoV-2.

The study confirms and complements findings from the Pfizer/BioNTech phase 3 randomized clinical trial. A parallel analysis assessed 25 potential adverse events in vaccinated versus unvaccinated individuals. Adverse events moderately associated with vaccination included lymph node swelling, appendicitis, and reactivation of herpes zoster, but these were rare.

"This study provides important information about the safety of COVID-19 vaccines in a real-world setting," said Reis. "The findings are consistent with the results of the phase 3 clinical trial and provide further evidence of the safety and effectiveness of these vaccines."

Balicer added, "The study shows that mRNA vaccines are safe and effective, and that the benefits of vaccination far outweigh the risks. We hope that these findings will help to alleviate concerns about vaccine safety and encourage more people to get vaccinated."
