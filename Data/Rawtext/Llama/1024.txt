Winter Storm Death Toll in Texas Rises to 210, with Fears of Undercounting

The Texas Department of State Health Services (DSHS) has updated the official winter storm death toll to 210, a sharp increase of 59 deaths from the previous tally. The majority of victims died from hypothermia, while vehicle crashes, carbon monoxide poisoning, and complications from chronic medical conditions also contributed to the fatalities.

The historic freeze in February left millions of Texans without power for days, crippling the state's electric grid and leaving many vulnerable to the deadly cold. Harris County reported the highest number of deaths at 43, followed by Travis and Dallas Counties with 28 and 20 deaths respectively.

However, the DSHS acknowledges that the current death toll may be an underestimation, as the tally only includes deaths that were directly attributed to the storm. Buzzfeed analysis suggests that the actual number of deaths could exceed 700, highlighting the potential for a significant undercount.

In response to the devastating storm, the Texas legislature passed legislation aimed at increasing the reliability of the state's power grid. However, some critics argue that the measures were insufficient, and Governor Greg Abbott faces criticism for not including grid reforms in the special legislative session agenda.

The winter storm highlighted significant deficiencies in Texas' electric system, raising questions about the state's preparedness for extreme weather events. As the state continues to grapple with the aftermath of the storm, there are growing calls for more comprehensive measures to ensure the safety and well-being of Texans in the face of future natural disasters.

The DSHS plans to continue updating the death tally as more information becomes available, providing a more accurate picture of the storm's devastating impact. In the meantime, communities across Texas are coming together to support those affected by the storm, with relief efforts ongoing.
