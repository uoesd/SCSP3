Ever Given's Owner Braces for Legal Storm After Suez Canal Blockage

The owner of the Ever Given, Shoei Kisen Kaisha Ltd., is bracing itself for a legal storm after the ship blocked the Suez Canal in March, disrupting global trade and sparking a flurry of potential claims. According to a London court hearing, the company expects to face hundreds or thousands of legal claims in the aftermath of the incident.

In an effort to streamline the legal process, a two-month stay on potential claims against the Ever Given was granted by the London court. This stay aims to group similar claims together, reducing legal costs and court time. A further court hearing is scheduled in two months to assess filed claims.

Stewart Buckingham, a court lawyer representing Shoei Kisen Kaisha Ltd., stated that the company is not liable for the grounding incident. "Our client's position is that they are not liable for the damage caused by the grounding of the vessel," he said.

Despite the owner's assertions of non-liability, an earlier court order limited some claims against the ship to 84 million pounds ($116 million). The ship's owners claim that there were some damages to the ship, but no injuries, deaths, or pollution were reported from the incident.

The resolution of any claims filed in the U.K. might take years and could involve arbitration. The legal process is expected to be complex and lengthy, with multiple parties involved and substantial amounts of money at stake.

The Ever Given, which is operated by Evergreen Group, blocked the Suez Canal for six days in March, causing a significant disruption to global trade. The incident has already had a major impact on the shipping industry, with many companies facing delays and increased costs due to the blockage.

The legal fallout from the incident is likely to continue for some time, with numerous parties potentially seeking compensation for losses incurred during the disruption. The stay in proceedings will allow for a more efficient handling of the claims, but the ultimate resolution of the legal disputes remains uncertain.
