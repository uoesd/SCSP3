Court Rules Creditors Can Keep $500 Million in Mistaken Citigroup Transfer

In a stunning turn of events, a New York federal judge has ruled that 10 creditors can keep nearly $500 million that was mistakenly transferred to them by Citigroup. The bank had intended to make an $8 million interest payment to the creditors, but instead, it accidentally sent $900 million of its own funds.

The case has sparked a debate about the responsibilities of creditors when a bank overpays, and raises questions about the principle of returning mistakenly sent money versus the right to use money repaid exactly as owed.

According to Judge Jesse Furman, the creditors had a reasonable belief that the payment was intentional due to its accuracy, and therefore, they could keep the money. The ruling was influenced by New York law, which aims to discourage banks from making such errors.

Adam Abensohn, representing the asset managers, argued that there is nothing unfair about keeping money that is owed. "The creditors did nothing wrong and were entitled to rely on the accuracy of the payment," he said.

The case began when Citigroup mistakenly transferred $900 million to the creditors instead of the $8 million interest payment. After the transfer, $400 million was returned by some creditors, but 10 others refused to return $500 million.

Judge Furman cited legal precedents in his ruling, stating that the creditors had a reasonable belief that the payment was intentional. He also noted that the bank's mistake was not the creditors' responsibility.
