ECB Joins Global Central Banks in Tapering QE, Lagarde Calls it a "Recalibration"

In a move that was widely expected, the European Central Bank (ECB) has announced that it will begin tapering its asset purchases, joining other major global central banks in reducing or ending quantitative easing (QE). The ECB's decision to recalibrate its asset purchases comes as the bank aims to navigate exiting QE without destabilizing the Eurozone.

According to ECB President Christine Lagarde, the bank's asset purchase rate has been significantly higher than the Federal Reserve's, reaching an average of $181 billion per month. "The ECB's decision to recalibrate its asset purchases is a necessary step in the normalization of our monetary policy," Lagarde said in a statement.

Other central banks, including the Bank of Japan, Bank of Canada, Bank of England, Reserve Bank of New Zealand, and Reserve Bank of Australia, have already started tapering or ending their QE programs. The ECB emphasized that its decision to reduce asset purchases is a "recalibration" rather than tapering, echoing similar stances taken by other central banks.

The Pandemic Emergency Purchase Program (PEPP) will see a "moderately lower" pace of net asset purchases, continuing at least until March 2022, with a balance level maintained at least until the end of 2023. Other ECB programs, such as the Targeted Longer-Term Refinancing Operations (TLTRO III) and the Asset Purchase Program (APP), will continue as planned.
