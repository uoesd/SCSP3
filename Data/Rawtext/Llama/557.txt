Western troops have struggled to control the massive crowds at the airport, leading to health and safety emergencies. The U.S. and British forces have airlifted thousands of people out of Afghanistan, but the effort is still below capacity.

In an effort to aid the evacuation of Afghan refugees, the U.S. has activated the Civil Reserve Air Fleet program, which allows commercial airlines to assist in the transportation of people and cargo during emergencies.

However, concerns of potential attacks by Islamic State affiliates have necessitated heightened security measures for military aircraft. "We are taking all necessary precautions to ensure the safety of our personnel and those we are evacuating," said U.S. Defense Secretary Lloyd Austin.

Despite the challenges, resistance against the Taliban is forming in parts of Afghanistan, with some regions pledging to defend against Taliban control. "We will not let the Taliban take over our country without a fight," said Amir Khan Motaqi, a senior Taliban official.

The Taliban have sought to reassure the public that they will not seek revenge against those who opposed them. "We want to assure all Afghans that we will not harm them," said Taliban spokesman Mohammad Naeem. "We are here to bring peace and stability to our country."

But many Afghans remain skeptical, and the situation at the airport remains tense. "I just want to get out of here and start a new life," said one Afghan man, who asked not to be named. "I don't know what the future holds, but I know I can't stay here."

As the evacuation efforts continue, the international community is watching with bated breath, hoping for a peaceful resolution to the crisis. "We are doing everything we can to help those in need," said U.S. National Security Adviser Jake Sullivan. "We will continue to work with our partners to ensure the safety and security of all Afghans."
