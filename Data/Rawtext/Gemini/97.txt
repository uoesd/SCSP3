Fraser's decisions aim to rebrand Citigroup as the "bank with a soul," improving its appeal to potential employees. The cultural and structural changes at Citigroup are part of a strategic plan Fraser will unveil in March, marking her first full year as CEO.

'Zoom-Free Fridays' and 'Hybrid' Work Model

Fraser's "Zoom-free Fridays" policy prohibits internal video meetings on Fridays, allowing employees to disconnect and focus on other tasks or personal time. She has also encouraged staff to take vacations and use unconventional working hours, such as starting earlier or later in the day.

In the post-pandemic period, Fraser has introduced a "hybrid" work model, allowing staff to work up to two days from home. This flexibility aims to accommodate employees' personal and family responsibilities while maintaining productivity.

Rebranding Citigroup as the 'Bank with a Soul'

Fraser's initiatives are part of a broader effort to rebrand Citigroup as a more employee-centric organization. She believes that by prioritizing work-life balance and flexibility, Citigroup can attract and retain top talent.

"We want to be the bank with a soul," Fraser said in an interview with CNN Business. "We want to be a place where people feel valued, respected, and supported."

Citigroup's Challenges and Fraser's Leadership

Citigroup has been underperforming in stock value compared to its competitors and faces regulatory pressure to overhaul its risk management and data systems. A significant blunder in August 2020 involved Citigroup erroneously wiring nearly $1 billion due to a mistake, costing the bank over $500 million.

Fraser has a history of managing crisis situations effectively, having dealt with challenges both in Citigroup's mortgage business and its Latin America operations. She is the first woman to lead a major American bank, and her appointment raises questions about the "glass cliff" phenomenon, where women are often appointed to leadership positions during times of crisis.

Analysts' Views on Fraser's Efforts

Bank analysts and investors view Fraser's efforts as necessary but insufficient without addressing deeper governance issues.

"Fraser is making some positive changes, but Citigroup has a long way to go," said Mike Mayo, a longtime banking analyst. "They need to improve their risk management and data systems, and they need to address the governance issues that have led to past problems."

Conclusion

Jane Fraser's leadership at Citigroup is a significant departure from the traditional Wall Street culture. Her focus on work-life balance, flexibility, and employee well-being is a bold move that could potentially reshape the industry. However, Citigroup faces significant challenges, and it remains to be seen whether Fraser's efforts will be enough to turn the bank's fortunes around.
