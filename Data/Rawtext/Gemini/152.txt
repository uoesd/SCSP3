Colin Powell, Trailblazing Secretary of State, Dies at 84 from COVID-19 Complications

Washington – Colin Powell, the first Black secretary of state and a towering figure in American foreign policy, died Monday from complications of COVID-19 at the age of 84.

Powell, who had been battling multiple myeloma, a blood cancer that affects the immune system, was fully vaccinated against COVID-19. His family announced his death in a statement, saying he had died "this morning due to complications from Covid 19."

President Joe Biden ordered flags at half-staff until October 22 in remembrance of Powell, whom he described as "a great man" and "a patriot of unmatched honor and dignity."

"Colin Powell was a dedicated public servant who served our nation with distinction and honor," Biden said in a statement. "He was a man of integrity, a man of character, and a man of wisdom."

A Trailblazing Career

Powell's career spanned more than half a century, during which he held some of the highest positions in the U.S. government. He was the first Black secretary of state, serving from 2001 to 2005 under President George W. Bush. He was also the first Black national security advisor, serving from 1987 to 1989 under President Ronald Reagan.

Powell's military career began in 1958, when he was commissioned as a second lieutenant in the U.S. Army. He served two tours of duty in Vietnam, where he was awarded the Bronze Star and the Purple Heart.

After retiring from the military in 1989, Powell served as chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff from 1989 to 1993 under President George H.W. Bush. In this role, he oversaw the U.S. military's operations during the Gulf War.
