The public caught a glimpse of these disturbing images last week when several videos were played in court during hearings for defendants charged in the riot. The footage, which includes surveillance footage and body camera recordings from police officers, reveals the extent of the mob's brutality and the overwhelming force they used against law enforcement.

Despite the gravity of the crimes committed, the Justice Department and federal courts have been reluctant to proactively release this footage, citing security concerns and the potential impact on fair trials. However, a coalition of media outlets, led by CNN, has formed to push for broader access to these videos, arguing that the public has a right to see the full extent of the violence that occurred.

"The public has a right to see what happened on January 6," said Drew Shenkman, a CNN lawyer. "These videos are essential to understanding the crimes that were committed and the threat to our democracy."

The videos shown in court have depicted the crowd's relentless violence, including law enforcement officers being beaten, trampled, and overwhelmed. One particularly disturbing video shows DC Police Officer Michael Fanone being dragged into the crowd and brutally assaulted.

"I was beaten, tased, and pepper-sprayed," Fanone testified in court. "I thought I was going to die."

The release of these videos has renewed discussions around the violence of Trump supporters toward police during the riot. Some defendants are contesting the release of videos, fearing it might affect their fair trial rights.

"The government has a duty to protect the rights of the accused," said Charles Tobin, an attorney for one of the defendants. "Releasing these videos could prejudice the jury pool."

However, media outlets argue that the public interest in understanding the full extent of the violence outweighs any potential prejudice to defendants.

"These videos are not just about the guilt or innocence of individual defendants," said Gabe Roth, executive director of Fix the Court. "They are about the health of our democracy and the need to hold accountable those who attacked our Capitol."

More than a dozen video access requests from the media coalition are still pending, with the Justice Department in some cases asking to block rebroadcasting. The courts are weighing the competing interests of the public's right to know and the defendants' fair trial rights.

"The court must balance the public's right to access information with the need to protect the fair trial rights of the defendants," said Judge Royce Lamberth.

Judge Michael Aloi, who is presiding over one of the Capitol riot cases, has expressed concern about the potential impact of releasing the videos.

"I'm not sure that the public needs to see every single video," Aloi said. "There are some videos that are so graphic that they could be prejudicial to the defendants."

However, Judge Emmet Sullivan, who is overseeing another Capitol riot case, has ruled that the public has a right to see the videos.

"The public has a right to know what happened on January 6," Sullivan said. "These videos are essential to understanding the events of that day."

Investigators continue to seek identification of more than 250 people believed to have been violent or assaulted police on January 6. The release of these videos is expected to aid in the ongoing investigation and help bring those responsible to justice.
