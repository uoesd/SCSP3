Impeachment Process

The impeachment process begins in the House of Representatives. A simple majority vote is required to impeach a president. The House Judiciary Committee will draft articles of impeachment, which will then be debated and voted on by the full House.

If the House votes to impeach the president, the Senate will hold a trial. The Senate will vote on whether to convict the president. A two-thirds majority vote is required to convict.

If the Senate convicts the president, he or she will be removed from office. The vice president will then become president.

Previous Impeachments

President Trump is only the third president in U.S. history to be impeached. President Andrew Johnson was impeached in 1868 for violating the Tenure of Office Act. President Bill Clinton was impeached in 1998 for perjury and obstruction of justice. Both Johnson and Clinton were acquitted by the Senate.

Impact of Impeachment

An impeachment conviction can have a significant impact on a president's legacy. President Clinton's impeachment led to a decline in his approval ratings and contributed to his decision not to run for re-election in 2000.

An impeachment conviction can also affect a president's ability to govern. President Johnson was impeached during a time of great political turmoil, and his impeachment made it difficult for him to pass legislation.
