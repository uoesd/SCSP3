Rittenhouse, who had become a national symbol during the trial, cried and hugged his lawyers after the jury's verdict was read, declaring him not guilty on multiple counts, including first-degree intentional homicide.

"I'm so relieved," Rittenhouse said outside the courthouse. "I've been waiting for this day for a long time."

The jury's decision accepted the defense's argument that Rittenhouse acted in self-defense when he shot three people amid unrest in Kenosha on August 25, 2020.

"We're very happy with the verdict," said Mark Richards, Rittenhouse's defense lawyer. "We believe that the jury got it right."

"This is a dangerous verdict," said Thomas Binger, the prosecutor. "It sends the message that you can bring a gun to a protest and shoot people if you feel threatened."

The verdict sparked strong reactions from political figures and the public, with opinions split along ideological lines.

Governor Gavin Newsom of California criticized the verdict, saying it "sends the wrong message" about vigilantism.

"This verdict is a miscarriage of justice," Newsom said. "It's a dangerous precedent that will allow vigilantes to roam our streets with impunity."

President Biden expressed concern about the verdict but urged for peaceful acceptance.

"While the verdict in Kenosha will leave many Americans feeling angry and concerned, myself included, we must acknowledge that the jury has spoken," Biden said. "I urge everyone to express their views peacefully."

Senator Ron Johnson, a Republican from Wisconsin, praised the verdict, saying it "affirms the right to self-defense."

"The jury saw through the prosecution's political agenda and made the right decision," Johnson said.

Rebecca Kleefisch, the Republican candidate for Wisconsin governor, also welcomed the verdict.

"This is a victory for the rule of law and for the Second Amendment," Kleefisch said.

Richards, Rittenhouse's defense lawyer, said his client looks forward to moving on with his life post-verdict.

"Kyle is a good kid who made a mistake," Richards said. "He's not a murderer. He's not a vigilante. He's just a kid who was trying to do the right thing."

The trial examined the events of August 25, 2020, when Rittenhouse, armed with an AR-15-style rifle and claiming to offer medical aid, shot three people amid unrest in Kenosha.

The prosecution argued that Rittenhouse provoked the violence by pointing his gun at protesters and that he was not justified in using deadly force.

The defense, however, argued that Rittenhouse acted in self-defense after being attacked by protesters.

The jury ultimately agreed with the defense's argument, finding that Rittenhouse reasonably believed that he was in imminent danger of being killed or seriously injured.

The verdict has reignited the national debate over gun rights, self-defense, and the role of vigilantes in society. It is a complex and controversial issue that will likely continue to be debated for years to come.
