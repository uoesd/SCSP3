Impeachment: A Constitutional Remedy for Trump's Abuse of Office

The American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU) firmly supports the impeachment of President Donald Trump. We believe that his actions, including his efforts to subvert the results of the 2020 election, constitute a clear and present danger to our democracy and warrant his removal from office.

The ACLU's board of directors unanimously passed a resolution calling for Trump's impeachment, emphasizing his "unprecedented and ongoing efforts to undermine the integrity of our democratic process." We believe that Trump's actions, including his speech on January 6th, 2021, in which he incited a mob to attack the U.S. Capitol, are part of a pattern of behavior that justifies his removal from office.

Impeachment is a political remedy, not a criminal proceeding. It is a process by which Congress can remove an executive official from office for abuse of office. The First Amendment does not protect public employees, including the president, from sanctions related to speech in their official capacity.

Impeachment does not require conviction of a crime but a determination of abuse of office warranting removal. The House of Representatives has the sole power to impeach, while the Senate has the sole power to try all impeachments. A two-thirds vote of the Senate is required to convict and remove an official from office.

The ACLU has a long history of defending free speech rights, regardless of the popularity of the speech. We believe that the First Amendment is essential to a free and democratic society. However, we also believe that the First Amendment does not protect speech that is likely to incite imminent lawless action.

The Supreme Court has held that the government may restrict speech that is likely to incite imminent lawless action. In Brandenburg v. Ohio, the Supreme Court established a test for determining whether speech is protected by the First Amendment. The Court held that speech is not protected if it is "directed at inciting or producing imminent lawless action" and is "likely to incite or produce such action."

We believe that Trump's speech on January 6th meets the Brandenburg test. His speech was clearly directed at inciting the mob to attack the Capitol. He told the crowd that they needed to "fight like hell" and that they would not "have a country anymore" if they did not stop the certification of the election results. His speech was also likely to incite violence. The crowd was already angry and agitated, and Trump's words pushed them over the edge.

We believe that Trump's actions on January 6th were a clear and present danger to our democracy. He incited a mob to attack the Capitol in an attempt to overturn the results of a free and fair election. His actions were a betrayal of his oath of office and a threat to our constitutional order.

We urge Congress to impeach and remove Trump from office. We believe that his actions warrant his removal and that it is necessary to protect our democracy.
